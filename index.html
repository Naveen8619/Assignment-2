    Interview Questions & Transcript

    Tell us about your IT work. What exactly do you do?

    I am a director of technology. This involves managing a multidisciplinary team of technology professionals. Roles such as developers, infrastructure professionals, support operators, project managers, business managers, solution designers, etc are the kind of roles you would find within the team. I liaise with business stakeholders to translate business needs into innovative technology solutions. I set technology roadmaps and strategies to deliver the organizational strategy and competitive advantage. I direct teams to deliver on strategic and tactical initiatives. My day to day involves multiple meetings with various stakeholders, to ensure timely information and direction. The role is not a hands-on technical role, with the main skills being stakeholder management, negotiation, strategy, business case development and other management skills, but of course a broad technology understanding of current and future technologies and how they may be best utilized is required.

    Please tell us about the industry you work in.

    It is a health-care regulation as part of a government agency. As a regulator, it is not profit driven, nor do competitors exist. IT delivery and business strategies are driven by regulatory need and risk to the public. Many projects and initiatives are driven out of government or public safety requirements. Being a government agency, decision making takes times and it is a very consultative environment (i.e., ideas and change needs to be heavily socialized and accepted before enacted).

    What other kinds of work do you have to do?

    I have got to do research, legal work (contract reviews and contract negotiations), run large procurement tenders, participate in industry events and conferences, responding to and writing lots of emails (hundreds a day). Also, I need to manage a team, conduct performance reviews, participate and conduct meetings, and run workshops.

    Who are all the different people you interact with in your work? Please tell us about them.

    My direct reports (enterprise manager, change and transition manager, service and deliver manager, property and sustainability manager), I liaise with them approximately 30% percent of my time to manage the teams deliverables (project work and BAU). These direct reports, manage the various areas of IT and the underlying technical specialists. For instance, the enterprise platform manager, manages application development and support, the test team, the business analysis team, digital services, and information management teams. 50% of my time, I work with business executives (i.e., the CEO, CFO, COO, etc), the managers of other departments of the business in order to maintain transparent and opened communications between IT and the business. It is through these interactions that requirements, strategies, and updates were exchanged and created. The remainder of the time (20%) is spent liaising with technical specialists, and vendors in order to exchange information on work engagements (planned or in-flight).

    Please tell us about your interactions with other IT professionals.

    The main interaction is with my direct reports. There will be various meetings established, each with its own agenda. Weekly one-on-one meetings are established, where I discuss specific team matters with each line manager. In the one-on-one meetings, the team performance, risks, challenges, and issues are discussed. There is a bi-directional exchange of information between the manager and I; they will provide options, issues, and ideas on the various matters, and an open discussion on the team and other matters and I provide updates from the executive, specific direction, and any required support to the manager. There are various fortnightly meetings established with a broader audience, which include all my line managers and other technical leads. These meetings are operational in nature and are there to maintain operational oversight of standard operating processors and management controls; basically, checking to see that things are working properly. In these meetings, various operational reports are walked through. In some of these meetings, approvals are invited for various changes to occur (example: changes to production systems, endorsing a solution design, approving a deviation from standard, etc). I interact with project managers, and business analysts in project status update meetings and steering committee meetings. I am the sponsor of the project, so I provide direction to the project. In steering committee meetings, for the projects that I may or may not be the sponsor of, engagement is more formal and is more about decision making for the project. I have monthly meetings with the entire team, where I communicate our cooperate direction and strategy, team matters, our progress to date, challenges and opportunities, and targets for the next period. These are some of the formal structured meetings I will have with IT professionals, but there is also daily informal communication: the corridor chat, the desk visit, the email or phone communication, etc. These are more quick decision or update kind of communications, or just a social interaction.

    What about your interactions with clients or investors?

    My clients are internal costumers. As I previously mentioned, 50% of my day is spent liaising with internal management. There are various forums where this occurs, from the formal meetings that are established (example: strategy workshop, townhall meetings, etc.) where everyone engages and provides updates on progress metrics or future plans to informal meetings, generally on a one on one basis, where I will periodically touch base with key stakeholders to ensure that their motivations, drivers, needs, concerns, and ideas are captured, listened to, and discussed. Doing this helps build rapport and trust between my clients and me and ensures, broadly speaking, IT delivery is on the right track. In terms of delivery itself, the team will liaise directly and work with the business customer to ensure the optimal outcomes (the business customer is part of the change, signs off on the requirement and the deliverable).

    What aspects of your work do you spend most time on? Please tell us about these.

    Most of my time is spent in meetings, as outlined in previous questions. My time is spent preparing for those meetings (preparing presentations, reviewing and commenting on documents, researching, preparing proposals, preparing reports, and communicating).

    Which aspects of your work do you find most challenging?

    Stakeholder management is the hardest part of the job. Communicating technical strategies and working with non-technical people to approve expenditure and deliver solutions, is very challenging. Just “keeping the lights on” requires significant investment, this is often lost on finance executives and other managers seeking to maximize their returns on investment. There is often a disconnect between IT and the rest of the business and it is the responsibility of IT management to educate and inform the business in order to bridge that gap. In a health regulatory environment this is quite challenging as management has a low maturity level of IT practices and needs, thus the divide and challenge is much greater.

    Finally, can you share an example of the work you do that best captures the essence of the IT industry?

    IT is about delivering change. IT professionals are change agents and no modern change is possible without it. Basically, IT professionals make the future happen. As an IT director, I have a role to play in every step of a change from the formation of the idea, having it approved, and its design to delivery of that idea and all the technical teams that need to come together to deliver it. It is my role that actually sees and works across the entire delivery landscape albeit at reduced detail. I am also held accountable should there be a failure in delivery.

    Cybersecurity

    The internet is like the sun, as the internet expands more problems and exploits are prevalent in recent society. But cybersecurity prevents those problems and exploits and includes the “measures used to protect the confidentiality, integrity and availability of systems and information.” (ACSC 2021, Glossary) It is important to be secure online as several threats are prevalent and lurking, these threats include: illegal crypto-mining, data spillage, DoS (Denial of service attacks), Hacking, Identify theft, Malicious insiders, Malware, Phishing, Ransomware, Scams and Web shell malware (ACSC 2021). As technology keeps advancing, new methods of security breaches appear. However, cybersecurity is still seeing an increase in demand and attacks are decreasing, according to Accenture “The total number of cyberattacks dropped 11 percent, from 232 to 206 targeted attacks.” (Accenture 2021, The basics seem better) The decrease of cyberattacks is also shown through “a larger drop of 27 percent in the number of security breaches which indicates the basics seem to be improving”. (Accenture 2021, The basics seem better). Despite this in 2019 “68% of business leaders [still] feel their cybersecurity risks are increasing” (Accenture 2019, New risks from innovation and growth). However Cybersecurity is a risk not limited to businesses, “A survey by the AIC found that identity crime 1 in 4 Australians have been a victim of identity crime at some point in their lives” (Australian Government Department of Home Affairs, 2021) and 68% of the victims have not reported the misuse of their information, this means that 17% of the Australian population has had their personal information misused without reporting it at all this is largely due to the lack of knowledge users have of cybersecurity, the AIC stated that 25.1% of the cases police did not do anything, 12.6% were too embarrassed to report it, 19.3% don’t know where to report it and 12.5% did not know that identify theft was a crime. According to the AIC only 32% of reports were accepted, despite each victim spending an average of 34 hours repairing the damage caused by identify theft. (AIC, 2021). And this is only one of the listed cyber-crimes above.

    And still despite this another huge presence is being developed, quantum computers, quantum computers have the potential to change the entire structure of the internet itself. Quantum computing overpowers classical computers as it utilizes qubits, whereas classic computers only use ones and zeros, qubits have a ‘superposition’ state that allows them to be a one and a zero. Allowing two qubits to represent four scenarios at the same time. (Brandmark, 2021) To put it in perspective Google’s Sycamore quantum processor calculated one million measurements on a quantum circuit, which took around 200s, which a state-of-the-art supercomputer would take 10,000 years to complete. (Physics world 2019, para. 4) Based on the processing speed of the quantum chips, both security and threat wise cybersecurity could be changed immensely. However, quantum computers are still far from becoming a possibility as problems such as quantum decoherence which comes from “aspects of the environment: changing magnetic and electric fields, radiation from warm objects, or cross talk between qubits” (Scientific American 2020, para. 3). Nonetheless IBM (International business Machines corporation) has still promised to develop numerous quantum computers over the next few years coming from the ‘Falcon’ – 27 qubits 2019, ‘Hummingbird’ – 65 qubits 2020, ‘Eagle’ – 127 qubits 2021, ‘Osprey’ – 433 qubits 2022 and ‘Condor’ – 1121 qubits in 2023. (See Figure 1) Figure 1: IBM Roadmap by IBM, 2020 

    A prime example of the importance of cyber security is quantum computing poses as it poses huge impact on cryptography, and in turn affects the crypto mining market heavily. For example, Bitcoin is a system for transferring value, a Bitcoin user is responsible for generating their own random address, and the user’s computer generates a random secret address that is required to transfer Bitcoin. (Deloitte, 2021) However a user with a quantum computer with access to another user’s public key could easily forge the private key and spend their Bitcoin which poses a huge problem for the crypto community. However, Quantum computers are not only threating crypto users but also the encryption and security of everyone on the internet is being threatened especially any type of asymmetric key pairs. Just Like how a quantum computer can crack a Bitcoin users private key, quantum computers can easily brute force and crack a password that uses factorization and prime numbers (havened, 2017) in 1 hour which would take a classic computer 78 million hours to do. It was also estimated that only 120 quantum computers were needed to disrupt every HTTPS connection in 2 seconds in 2017. 

    Cyber-security goes hand in hand with the advancement of technology and as technology keeps advancing so will the jobs related to it. Australia’s cyber security growth network sates that the government has spent 9% more per year and employed 4000 workers per year since 2017. The ASCS found that “90% of Australian organizations faced some form of attempted or successful cyber security compromise” (Seek 2021, para. 1). Cybersecurity affects everything and everyone online ensuring that companies, governments and individual’s information is kept private and secure. 

    Cybersecurity is an important part of everyone’s life, just as people lock their houses and cars to file cabinets and safes, they should lock their phones and information online, almost all information nowadays has an online copy, and an insecure lock could lead to a leak in information. Cybersecurity is prevalent is everyone’s daily lifestyle as users unlock their phones and computers, they also login to websites and applications posing way more information to be kept secret and as more information needs to be secured a large demand in cybersecurity employment for me as an individual is a welcome opportunity as it creates more positions in my ideal job. 

    Cybersecurity also creates an opportunity for more efficient data protection than physical security with thousands of “hidden” locks to be created to protect sensitive data. This affects everyone online as anyone who has access to a computer can encrypt and secure data to prevent access to anyone else. Despite this however many do not protect their online information as much as they should, which is portrayed through the massive identity thieving online. Myself, my family and friends often share passwords to several services and unlike creating copies of a physical key or sharing even more sensitive information so they can access the service, it is much easier and safer to do. Cybersecurity has already become an essential part in modern society and therefore has already affected everyone around me, but the advancement in security will create a more secure, efficient and effective method of information encryption and security.

    CLOUDS, SERVICES, SERVERS

    Clouds, services, and servers look like three completely different things on the surface. In the IT industry cloud refers to in simple terms, the internet but using just software rather than software and hardware. Services refer to tasks that are completed with the assistance of technology. Finally, servers simply store, send, and receive data. However, what do these three things have in common… Cloud Computing.     BLOCKCHAINS & CRYPTOCURRENCY

    A blockchain is a chain of blocks that contain information. This technology can be dated back to 1991 where a group of researchers created it for the use of timestamping digital documents, so that it not possible to tamper with those documents. This technology of blockchains went by mostly unused until 2009 when Satoshi Nakamoto created the digital cryptocurrency Bitcoin. A blockchain can be described as a digital ledger that is open to everyone, and anyone can add to it, but no one can change it. 

    This interesting property of blockchains of not being able to be altered is why it is slowly being adopted by many different companies across different fields and different countries. But how does this work? Each block in a blockchain has three main components. It contains data, a hash and the hash of the previous block. The data stored in each block will differ, depending on the type of blockchain it is on. For example, the data in a block in Bitcoin will contain information on a transaction. This will include the sender, receiver, amount of Bitcoin sent and the date and time. A hash is something similar to a digital fingerprint and will identify a block and all of its contents and will always be unique. Because the blocks hash can identify its contents and data, changing the information on a block will change its hash, meaning the block is no longer the same block. This helps to identify change on the blockchain. Finally, a block will also contain the hash of a previous block. This creates the ‘chain’ element of a blockchain.

    Because each block has its own hash and the hash of a previous block, this means that if someone attempts to change data on a block, the hash of that singular block is changed. However, the next block on the blockchain will now hold an invalid hash of the previous block and the next block after that too. Changing the data of a singular block will cause the rest of the blockchain to be invalid and the system will know something is wrong. A problem in the blockchain is easily identifiable because it can be traced to exactly where in the blockchain a problem has started. This system is very secure, however these days, the hash system alone cannot protect against all tampering and hacks. Computers these days can tamper with one block and calculate hundreds or even thousands of hashes for the following blocks, making the blockchain valid. 

    Today, we utilize two more components that make the blockchain that is as secure as it is today. Blocks in blockchains have a mechanism called proof-of-work. This mechanism slows down the creation of new blocks, as each new block will also be ‘certified’ with a proof-of-work. Now trying to tamper with a blockchain not only includes having to recalculate hundreds or thousands of new hashes but will need to certify all these new blocks as well. Finally, blockchains will also be distributed. Unlike many other servers or programs where a central entity or computer will manage a system, blockchains use a peer-to-peer network that anyone is allowed to join. When a person joins, they receive a full copy of the blockchain, called a node. When a new block is now created, the block is sent to everyone on the blockchain and each node will verify the block to make sure it has not been tampered with. Once the block has been verified, each node will accept this block and add it to their blockchain. Nodes will communicate with each other to come to a consensus. They agree on which blocks are valid and which are not. To successfully tamper on a modern blockchain, a hacker would have to recalculate a hash and certify every following block on the blockchain a proof-of-work and would need to do this across over half of the peers on the network. This becomes almost impossible to do once even just a couple people join the network, making blockchains very secure.

    One of the big factors in the popularization of blockchain technology is its decentralized nature. No one big entity controls the blockchain, instead all the peers do. One of the biggest uses of blockchain technology stems from this factor. Currencies such as the US dollar (USD) or the Australian dollar (AUD) are centralized- there is a central authority that will effectively control the value and distribution of currency, in the case of the USD and AUD, the US and Australian governments. There is nothing to stop the governments from printing out more money, inflating the market and in turn, devalue their respective currency. In fact, due to the ongoing effects of the corona virus, many governments have printed more money in the form of stimulus checks reducing the value of their currency. This is where cryptocurrencies come in. Cryptocurrencies are essentially digital tokens created from code, all of which were created and exchanged on a blockchain. This allows for any user across the globe to exchange funds via almost instantaneous transactions with absolute security. Bitcoin and many other cryptocurrencies were created with a limited supply in mind- that only a certain amount of the currency would ever be in existence to prevent inflation. Other cryptocurrencies such as Ethereum employed a mining cap of 18 million tokens per year. 

    Over the past few years since the popularization of Bitcoin, people have realized that blockchain technology could have multitude of potential use cases. The technology is immutable, efficient, transparent and offers security and traceability to businesses. The data stored can be used for just about anything from inventory management to media licensing and will include greater security and convenience. Take transactions for example- usually to transfer an amount of money, banks will withhold the money transferred for up to 24 hours, until those funds arrive at the receiver’s account. Payments to companies for goods and services can be ‘pending’ even longer, up to 5 working days. Without the use of an intermediatory on a blockchain, funds can be sent and settled within a matter of seconds. Another potential use case could be movement of in person or mail voting to digital voting. The recent 2020 US presidential election had many people doubting the authenticity of the vote results, however this could be avoided from occurring again if the vote could be set up on a system with a blockchain implementation. Votes could be traced, and it would be impossible to tamper with results. Many healthcare systems have already been moving away from paper record keeping and are developing blockchain systems to manage their medical data.  

    Blockchain technology will be the future of storing and sharing data. Companies and corporations from all sectors are already moving to a blockchain system, drawn by its security and functionality. In future, all types of records and transactions could be stored on a blockchain. Courthouse minutes could be uploaded to a blockchain, warehouses could update their sales and inventory on a blockchain, even university assignment submissions could take place on a blockchain. Our society is moving toward a digital world, where daily activities like education, shopping and taxes and bills can all be done in the comfort of our home in front of our screens. When so many of our activities are all online, there is an only a growing concern for the safety of our digital identities. Why not implement a blockchain system into these activities so that what we do is more convenient and so that we can have reassurance that our activities are more secure? Blockchains are already on the way to becoming a foundation to our generation’s technological world. Cryptocurrencies making a start to becoming a world-wide currency that consumers can used to purchase products. Right now, there are thousands of products and services being created around blockchains that can provide us with solutions for our changing needs. It may take a while, but in 5 years, 10 years, blockchains will become an integral part of our society.

    Overview

    Thousands of animals are euthanized each year in the USA and Australia alone. This application seeks to reduce those numbers. “SAFE RAP” will be both an Android and IOS application targeted towards those who are looking to adopt a pet and/or help contribute to shelter animals. This application will also be catered for Animal shelters and other organisations that deal with abandoned pets, allowing them to put the animal up for adoption via the application. This application will primarily run similar to a ‘dating app’, in which the animals (primarily dogs and cats) will be put on the page with a small bio, to which the users are able to swipe left or right depending on which they are interested in. Information is exchanged as well as any payment for animals rescued. Users will also be able to donate to the animal shelters.

    Motivation

    The number of dogs and cats put in shelters and euthanized each year is alarmingly high. “Each year, approximately 1.5 million shelter animals are euthanized (670,000 dogs and 860,000 cats)” in the U.S. alone (Pet Statistics 2015, para. 2) with a total of “28,072” dogs, and “45,364” cats being brought into the RSPCA in the 2019-2020 financial year (RSPCA 2021, para. 3) . (Figure 5)

    Whilst this application may not solve the problem at hand, it gives the animals a higher chance of being rescued than the currently have, it gives those looking to purchase a pet an alternate option, as well as highlight animal shelters and the issue to the public. For instance, if we could just save ten percent of dogs given up in the USA alone, we have the potential to save around 67,000 animals, which is still incredible. (Figure 6, a dog waiting to be rescued)

    Description

    This application will be primarily targeted to those who are looking to adopt a pet and/or help contribute to shelter animals, individuals who are wanting to put their pet up for adoption, as well as animal shelters and other organisations that deal with abandoned pets.

    Upon opening the application, the user (adopter) will be required to sign up and create their own personal profile, including their full name, location, living conditions (do they have a back yard or not?) and finally their preferred animal being either a dog or a cat as well as its sex, breed, colour, size, etc.

    On the other hand, if the user is looking to put their animal up for adoption, they must also include pictures of their animal to show potential adopters, proof of ownership over the animal (e.g., a microchip and documentation details). This is to prevent fraud from taking place throughout the processes (only verified users will be able to put animals up for adoption, ownership details verified at exchange with the animal).

    Once finishing the sign up and profile, users will be sent to a page of animals listed within their location and matching the preferences initially described. Users are then given the option to swipe left (dismiss) or right (shortlist) depending on whether the bio and pictures of the matched animal profiles spark their interests.

    Furthermore, users will be able to make a contribution through the donation button, even if they do not adopt an animal, in which they will be allowed to send any sum of money to a registered shelter (not individuals) in order to support those organisations. However, this donation feature will be present when contributing to an animal shelter, meaning that an individual putting their pet for adoption will not be able to receive any donations/nor will an individual be able to send donations, as these contributions will be only available for the animal shelters.

    After deciding to swipe right for example, the listed animal will go on to their profiles shortlist. From there, they can complete the transaction (like a check-out on eBay). The animal profile would already have contact information for the shelter so inquiries about the animal and/or how to proceed with the transaction (will they go to pick up the animal, or will it be dropped off to their property?) could be done offline. This is the area in which the two individuals can communicate and discuss any personal requests or ask further questions regarding the animal. Additionally, in terms of payment, an individual purchasing an animal will also be given the option to ‘pay on pickup’. Similarly, to ‘Facebook Marketplace’, an individual can choose to pay when picking up their chosen animal as opposed to through the application.

    However, as this is a high-level project, we will need to further socialise through the use of surveys amongst the general public, as well as gaining more insight as to how animal shelters operate, in order to gain a better and more in depth understanding of what other features would be expected to implement into the application. This would be done with the aim of raising the overall user experience and the quality of the final product.
